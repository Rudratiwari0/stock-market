02 Foundation

What is the Stock Market?

The stock market is simply a place where people buy and sell small pieces of companies. These small pieces are called shares or stocks.

🏪 Simple example

Imagine a chai shop needs ₹1,00,000 to expand. Instead of a bank loan, the owner sells 1,000 pieces (shares) at ₹100 each. You buy 10 shares for ₹1,000 — now you own 1% of the chai shop. If the shop becomes worth ₹2,00,000 — your shares are now worth ₹2,000. That's how stocks work.

📊 How the stock market works

Key terms you must know

  • Share / Stock — A small ownership piece of a company
  • Shareholder — A person who owns shares of a company
  • Market Price — The current price at which a share is being bought/sold
  • Profit (Capital Gain) — When you sell a share higher than you bought it
  • Loss — When you sell a share lower than you bought it
  • Dividend — Some companies share a part of their profit with shareholders (like a bonus)
  • Portfolio — The full collection of all stocks you own

Why do share prices go up or down?

  • More people want to buy a stock → price rises ↑
  • More people want to sell a stock → price falls ↓
  • Strong company results, positive news, or economic growth → price tends to go up
  • Bad results, scandals, or economic uncertainty → price tends to go down

Who are the key players?

  • Companies — List their shares on the exchange to raise money
  • Retail Investors — Normal people like you and me investing small amounts
  • Institutional Investors — Big organisations like mutual funds and insurance companies
  • Brokers — Middlemen like Zerodha, angelone, Groww, or Upstox that let you buy/sell
  • SEBI — The Securities and Exchange Board of India. The regulator that makes rules, investigates fraud, and protects investors
📌 Important

When you buy a share, you become a part-owner of that company. You make money when it does well — and lose money when it doesn't. Stock investing always carries risk.

03 India Specific

NSE & BSE — India's Two Stock Exchanges

India has two major stock exchanges where shares are bought and sold every day — NSE and BSE. Think of them like two big marketplaces for stocks.

  • BSE stands for Bombay Stock Exchange — established in 1875, it is Asia's oldest stock exchange, located in Mumbai
  • NSE stands for National Stock Exchange — established in 1992, it is India's largest exchange by trading volume
  • Both are regulated by SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)
  • Most big companies are listed on both exchanges
🏬 Simple analogy

Think of NSE and BSE like two different shopping malls selling the same brands (companies). You can buy Reliance shares from either mall — the price is almost the same. NSE is the bigger, busier mall today.

NSE vs BSE — Quick comparison

Feature NSE BSE
Full Name National Stock Exchange Bombay Stock Exchange
Founded 1992 1875
Location Mumbai Mumbai
Main Index NIFTY 50 SENSEX
No. of Listed Companies ~2,000+ ~5,000+
Trading Volume Higher (more popular) Lower
Used for Stocks, F&O, Currency Stocks, SME trading

Which one should beginners use?

  • For most beginners, NSE is the go-to exchange — higher liquidity means easier buying and selling
  • Your broker app (Zerodha, Groww, etc.) lets you choose which exchange to trade on
  • For large-cap stocks, the price difference between NSE and BSE is tiny (fractions of a rupee)
📌 Did you know?

BSE's SENSEX tracks the top 30 companies. NSE's NIFTY 50 tracks the top 50 companies. Both are used as a health indicator of the Indian economy.

04 Index

NIFTY 50 — India's Most Famous Index

NIFTY 50 is a number that tells you how India's top 50 companies are performing right now. When people say "the market is up today" — they usually mean NIFTY 50 went up.

🌡️ Real-life analogy

Think of NIFTY 50 like a class result average. If the class average goes up, most students did well. If it goes down, most students struggled. NIFTY 50 is the average performance of India's top 50 companies.

What exactly is NIFTY 50?

  • It is a stock market index maintained by NSE — the National Stock Exchange of India
  • It tracks the top 50 companies listed on NSE, chosen by market size (largest companies)
  • These 50 companies span 13 different sectors — banking, IT, pharma, energy, FMCG, and more
  • The index is reviewed every 6 months — underperforming companies can be removed, stronger ones added
  • It started at a base value of 1,000 in November 1995 — it has since grown many-fold (to approximately 22,000+ as of recent years), reflecting India's long-term economic growth

Some companies in NIFTY 50 right now

Reliance TCS HDFC Bank Infosys ICICI Bank Wipro SBI Bajaj Finance HUL Tata Motors Asian Paints Maruti L&T Sun Pharma Adani Ports + 35 more
🏭 NIFTY 50 — key sectors represented

How is NIFTY 50 calculated?

  • It uses a method called Free-Float Market Capitalisation — bigger companies have more impact on the index
  • Reliance, TCS, and HDFC Bank are among the biggest — if their price moves, NIFTY moves a lot
  • If all 50 stocks go up → NIFTY goes up. If most fall → NIFTY goes down
📈 Real number example

If NIFTY 50 is at approximately 22,500 today and goes to around 23,000 tomorrow, that's a rise of ~500 points (~2.2%). Your mutual fund or index fund that tracks NIFTY would also grow by roughly 2.2%.

Why does NIFTY 50 matter to you?

  • It shows the overall mood of the Indian stock market at a glance
  • Most mutual funds and index funds in India track NIFTY 50
  • When NIFTY rises over years, long-term investors grow their wealth
  • Traders use NIFTY to take positions in Futures & Options (F&O)
  • It is used by the government and RBI as an economic health indicator
💡 NIFTY vs SENSEX

NIFTY 50 = NSE's index of top 50 companies. SENSEX = BSE's index of top 30 companies. Both move in the same direction most of the time. NIFTY is more widely used for trading and analysis in India today.

5.1 Mechanics

How Buying & Selling Works

Buying a stock is simpler than it sounds. You pick a company, place an order through an app, and in seconds — you own a small piece of that company.

Step-by-step: buying your first share

  • Step 1 — Open a Demat + Trading account with a broker like Zerodha, AngelOne, Groww, or Upstox
  • Step 2 — Add money to your account via UPI or net banking
  • Step 3 — Search for a stock — e.g., type "TCS" in the app
  • Step 4 — Place a Buy order — choose how many shares and at what price
  • Step 5 — Order executes — the stock exchange matches your buy with someone else's sell
  • Step 6 — Shares land in your Demat account within T+1 day (next trading day)
📦 Real example

Say a TCS share is trading at around ₹3,800 (example price). You buy 2 shares → you pay approx. ₹7,600. If TCS rises to around ₹4,000, your 2 shares are now worth ~₹8,000 — a profit of approx. ₹400. To book that profit, you simply sell the shares.

What happens behind the scenes?

  • Your order goes to the stock exchange (NSE or BSE)
  • The exchange finds a seller willing to sell at your price — this is called order matching
  • Money moves from your account to the seller; shares move from the seller to you
  • This whole process is called settlement — in India it takes T+1 day (trade day + 1 working day)
💡 T+1 Settlement

If you buy shares on Monday, the shares will officially be in your Demat account by Tuesday. This is what "T+1" means — trade date plus 1 working day. You can still sell on the same day though — that's called intraday trading.

⚡ Your order — step by step

Bid price vs Ask price

  • Bid price — the highest price a buyer is willing to pay right now
  • Ask price — the lowest price a seller is willing to accept right now
  • The difference between them is the spread — usually very small for popular stocks
🔢 Quick example

Say Infosys has a Bid of ₹1,500 | Ask of ₹1,501 (example). If you place a market buy order, you'll buy at ₹1,501 (the ask). If you sell, you'll get ₹1,500 (the bid). The ₹1 gap is the spread.

5.2 Account

Demat Account

Just like a bank account holds your money, a Demat account holds your shares in digital (electronic) form. "Demat" is short for Dematerialised — meaning no physical share certificates.

🏦 Simple analogy

Think of a Demat account like your locker at the bank. When you buy shares of Reliance, those shares get stored in your Demat locker digitally. When you sell, they leave the locker. You never touch a paper certificate.

Three accounts you actually need

  • Bank Account — holds your money (you already have this)
  • Trading Account — used to place buy/sell orders on the exchange
  • Demat Account — stores the shares you own
🏦 How your three accounts connect
💡 Good news

When you sign up with a broker like Zerodha, Groww, or Angel One, they open all three accounts together in one go. You don't need to visit a bank or CDSL/NSDL separately.

Who maintains Demat accounts in India?

  • NSDL (National Securities Depository Ltd) — linked to NSE
  • CDSL (Central Depository Services Ltd) — linked to BSE
  • Your broker connects to one of these two. Your shares are safely held there — even if your broker shuts down, your shares are safe

What can a Demat account hold?

  • Stocks — shares of companies like TCS, Reliance, HDFC Bank
  • Mutual Fund units
  • ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds)
  • Bonds and Government Securities
5.3 Players

Market Participants

The stock market isn't just you and a computer. Many types of players buy and sell every day — each with different goals, money sizes, and strategies.

1. Retail Investors (That's you!)

  • Individuals like you and me who invest their own personal money
  • Usually trade in small quantities — buying 5, 10, or 50 shares at a time
  • Use apps like Groww, AngelOne, Zerodha, Upstox to place orders
  • Make up a large number of market participants but a smaller share of total traded value
👤 Example

Rahul, a software engineer in Pune, buys 10 shares of HDFC Bank using the AngelOne app every month as part of his SIP. He is a retail investor.

2. Institutional Investors (The big fish)

  • Large organisations that invest crores and thousands of crores of rupees
  • Mutual Funds — collect money from lakhs of retail investors and invest it together
  • FIIs / FPIs (Foreign Institutional Investors) — overseas funds investing in India (e.g. BlackRock, Vanguard)
  • DIIs (Domestic Institutional Investors) — Indian entities like LIC, SBI Mutual Fund
  • When FIIs sell heavily, markets often fall. When they buy, markets rise. Watch FII/DII data to understand big moves.
📊 Why it matters

If you see news saying "FIIs sold ₹3,000 crore worth of shares today" — that's why NIFTY fell 300 points. Big institutions move the market. Retail investors mostly follow the trend.

3. Brokers (The middlemen)

  • Brokers are the gateway between you and the stock exchange
  • You cannot directly trade on NSE or BSE — you must go through a SEBI-registered broker
  • Discount brokers — low cost, app-based: Zerodha, Groww, Upstox, Angel One
  • Full-service brokers — offer advice + research, higher fees: ICICI Direct, HDFC Securities
  • Brokers earn through brokerage fees — a small amount charged per trade
🔁 How it flows

You open AngelOne (broker) → tap Buy on Infosys → AngelOne sends your order to NSE → NSE matches it with a seller → shares come to your Demat account. AngelOne charges you a tiny fee for this service.

6.1 Execution

Order Types

When you tap "Buy" on your broker app, you need to choose what kind of order to place. The three most important ones are Market Order, Limit Order, and Stop Loss.

1. Market Order — Buy/Sell right now

  • You say: "Buy this stock immediately at whatever price it's trading at"
  • Order fills instantly — you don't wait
  • Best for large, liquid stocks where price won't jump much
  • Downside: you don't control the exact price you pay
📈 NIFTY example

Say NIFTY is around 24,200 (example). You place a market buy order on a NIFTY ETF. Your order fills almost immediately at roughly ₹24,202 — slightly higher because of the spread and real-time price movement. You got in fast, but not at an exact price.

2. Limit Order — Buy/Sell only at your price

  • You say: "Buy only if the price comes down to ₹X" (or sell only if it rises to ₹X)
  • Your order sits in the queue and waits — it may or may not fill
  • You get price control but no guarantee the order will execute
  • Best when you have a specific entry price in mind
📉 NIFTY example

Say NIFTY is around 24,200 but you want to buy only at 24,000. You place a limit buy at 24,000. If NIFTY dips to 24,000, your order fires automatically. If it never reaches 24,000 that day, your order expires unfilled.

3. Stop Loss Order — Protect yourself from big losses

  • You already own a stock. You set a trigger price below your buy price
  • If the stock falls to that price, it automatically sells — cutting your loss
  • Think of it as an emergency exit that works even when you're not watching
  • Every serious trader uses stop losses — it is non-negotiable
🛡️ NIFTY example

Say you buy a NIFTY ETF at around ₹24,000. You place a stop loss at ₹23,700 (approx. 1.25% below). If NIFTY crashes to 23,700, your ETF is sold automatically. You lose roughly ₹300 per unit — but you avoid a much larger loss if the crash continues.

Quick comparison

Order Type When it fills Price control Best for
Market Order Immediately None — fills at current price Fast entry/exit
Limit Order Only at your set price Full control Planned entry
Stop Loss When price hits trigger Protects downside Risk management
💡 Beginner tip

Start with limit orders for buying — you pick your price and wait. Always set a stop loss after every buy. Avoid market orders in low-volume stocks — the price can slip unexpectedly.

6.2 Pricing

Bid & Ask Price

Every stock has two prices at the same time — a Bid price and an Ask price. Understanding these two numbers helps you buy and sell smarter.

What they mean

  • Bid Price — the highest price a buyer is willing to pay right now
  • Ask Price (also called Offer Price) — the lowest price a seller is willing to accept right now
  • The difference between them is called the Spread
  • A trade happens when a buyer agrees to the ask, or a seller agrees to the bid
📊 NIFTY ETF example

A NIFTY BeES ETF might show: Bid ₹240.50 | Ask ₹240.60 (example). The spread is ₹0.10. If you place a market buy, you'll pay ₹240.60 (the ask). If you place a market sell, you'll get ₹240.50 (the bid). That ₹0.10 is the hidden cost of a fast trade.

📊 Bid vs Ask — visualised

Why the spread matters

  • A tight spread (small gap) = easy to trade, lower cost. Common in NIFTY 50 stocks like TCS, Reliance
  • A wide spread (large gap) = harder to trade, higher hidden cost. Common in small-cap or illiquid stocks
  • When you buy at Ask and immediately sell at Bid, you lose the spread — that's why frequent intraday traders watch spreads closely
💡 Simple rule

Stick to NIFTY 50 stocks as a beginner — they have very tight spreads (often just 1 paisa to 50 paise). Avoid obscure small-cap stocks where the spread can be ₹5 to ₹20 wide.

6.3 Activity

Volume

Volume is simply the total number of shares traded in a stock during a given time period (a day, an hour, or even a minute). It tells you how active a stock is.

📦 Real example

On a normal day, Reliance Industries might trade 80 lakh shares. On a day when Reliance announces big news, volume might spike to 2 crore shares. That spike tells you: something important is happening — big players are reacting.

What high volume means

  • High volume + price rising → strong buying interest — the move is likely genuine and sustainable
  • High volume + price falling → heavy selling pressure — the fall may continue further
  • Low volume + price rising → weak, unconvincing move — often reverses quickly. Be cautious

Volume and NIFTY

  • When NIFTY rises 200 points on high volume, it signals broad market confidence
  • When NIFTY rises 200 points on low volume, it may just be light buying — not a strong trend
  • Traders always check "is volume confirming the move?" before entering a trade
📈 NIFTY volume example

Say NIFTY jumps from around 24,000 to 24,400 (up ~400 points, as an example). Check NSE data — if total market volume is 2× the average, this is a strong, trusted rally. If volume is below average, experienced traders call it a "low conviction move" and stay cautious.

Where to check volume

  • On any stock chart — volume bars appear at the bottom of every chart
  • Tall bars = high volume. Short bars = low volume
  • Apps like Zerodha Kite, TradingView, NSE website all show volume clearly
💡 Beginner rule of thumb

Never buy a stock that is rising on unusually low volume — it often reverses. Always prefer stocks with consistent, healthy daily volume (at least a few lakh shares per day).

Part 2 — Technical Analysis

7.1 Charting

Charts Basics

A chart is simply a picture of price over time. Instead of looking at a boring table of numbers, a chart lets you see where a stock or index has been — and helps you guess where it might go.

What is a chart?

  • The X-axis (horizontal) shows time — minutes, hours, days, months
  • The Y-axis (vertical) shows price — in rupees
  • Every point or bar on the chart = the price at that moment in time
  • Charts are the main tool used in Technical Analysis — reading price patterns to make trading decisions
📺 Simple analogy

Think of a chart like a temperature graph on a weather app. The line going up means it got hotter. Going down means cooler. A stock chart is the same idea — line going up means price went up, going down means it fell.

Type 1 — Line Chart

  • The simplest chart — just a single line connecting closing prices each day
  • Easy to read at a glance — great for seeing the overall trend
  • Does not show how much the price moved within a day — only where it closed
  • Best for: long-term investors who want to see the big picture
📈 NIFTY 50 — Line Chart (5 days)

NIFTY dipped on Tuesday, then recovered strongly — clearly visible on a line chart.

📉 NIFTY line chart example

Open NIFTY on TradingView and switch to a Weekly line chart. You'll instantly see that NIFTY went from ~17,000 in early 2023 to ~24,000 by 2024. That upward sloping line = a healthy bull market. No candles needed to see that.

Type 2 — Candlestick Chart

  • The most popular chart used by traders worldwide
  • Each "candle" shows 4 prices for that time period: Open, High, Low, and Close (OHLC)
  • Green candle = price went up (Close > Open). Red candle = price went down (Close < Open)
  • The thin lines above/below the body are called wicks (or shadows) — they show the high and low
  • Best for: traders who need to see price movement in detail
🕯️ Anatomy of a single candle
🕯️ NIFTY candlestick example

NIFTY on a Monday: Opens at 24,100, goes as high as 24,350, dips to 24,050, closes at 24,280. Result: a green candle. Body goes from 24,100 (open) to 24,280 (close). Top wick reaches 24,350. Bottom wick touches 24,050. One candle — four pieces of information.

Line vs Candlestick — which to use?

Feature Line Chart Candlestick Chart
Shows Only closing price Open, High, Low, Close
Best for Long-term trend view Short-term trading
Complexity Very simple Moderate
Used by Investors, beginners Traders, analysts
💡 Beginner recommendation

Start by looking at a daily candlestick chart of NIFTY 50 on TradingView. Each candle = one full trading day. Green days = NIFTY went up. Red days = NIFTY fell. Once you can read this comfortably, you're ready to learn patterns.

7.2 Time

Timeframes

A timeframe controls how much time each candle (or data point) on a chart represents. The same stock looks completely different on a 5-minute chart vs a weekly chart — and both are showing you the truth, just at different zoom levels.

Common timeframes explained

  • 1 min / 5 min / 15 min — each candle = that many minutes of trading. Used by intraday traders who open and close trades the same day
  • 1 Hour (1H) — each candle = 1 hour of trading. Good for swing traders holding positions for a few days
  • Daily (1D) — each candle = one full trading day. The most widely used timeframe — good for everyone
  • Weekly (1W) — each candle = one full week. Used by long-term investors to see the big picture
  • Monthly (1M) — each candle = one month. Used to study multi-year trends
🔍 NIFTY zoom-in example

On a 5-minute chart, NIFTY might look volatile — bouncing up and down every few candles. But zoom out to the Daily chart and you see NIFTY is clearly in an uptrend. The 5-min noise disappears. Always check the daily chart before making any decision.

Which timeframe should you use?

You are a… Recommended Timeframe Why
Complete beginner Daily (1D) Clear signals, less noise, easy to follow
Intraday trader 5 min + 15 min Fine-grained price action for same-day trades
Swing trader 1H + Daily Spots multi-day setups
Long-term investor Weekly + Monthly Ignores short-term noise, sees real trend

The golden rule — higher timeframe wins

  • If the daily chart says NIFTY is in a downtrend, don't fight it just because the 5-min chart looks bullish
  • Always check the bigger timeframe first, then zoom in for your entry
  • This is called Top-Down Analysis — used by professional traders every day
🗓️ NIFTY top-down example

Weekly chart: NIFTY in a clear uptrend — higher highs, higher lows over months. Daily chart: NIFTY pulled back 2% this week. 15-min chart: Now bouncing off support. A swing trader sees this as a buying opportunity — the short-term dip within a larger uptrend.

💡 Where to practice

Go to TradingView.com → search "NIFTY 50" → switch between timeframes using the buttons at the top (1D, 1W, 1H etc.). Try switching from Daily to 5-min on any big news day and see how the same move looks completely different at each zoom level.

08 Price Action

Candlestick Patterns

A candlestick pattern is a shape formed by one or more candles that tells you what buyers and sellers are doing. Certain shapes repeat again and again — and they often signal that the price is about to reverse or continue.

Candlestick Basics — a quick recap

  • Body — the thick part. Shows the distance between open and close
  • Wick (shadow) — the thin lines above and below the body. Show how far price moved before coming back
  • Green candle — price closed higher than it opened (buyers won)
  • Red candle — price closed lower than it opened (sellers won)
🕯️ Quick example

Reliance opens at ₹2,800, goes up to ₹2,850, falls to ₹2,780, then closes at ₹2,820. The candle body goes from ₹2,800 to ₹2,820 (green). Top wick = ₹2,850. Bottom wick = ₹2,780. Buyers were stronger overall.

Pattern 1 — Doji

A Doji has almost no body — open and close are nearly the same. It means buyers and sellers are equal in strength. Nobody won. This is a sign of indecision. After a big move up or down, a Doji often warns that the trend might pause or reverse.

📌 Doji example

NIFTY has been going up for 5 days. On day 6, it opens at 24,400, goes up to 24,430, dips to 24,370, then closes at 24,402. Tiny body, long wicks on both sides. That's a Doji — bulls are running out of energy. Watch for a pullback.

Pattern 2 — Hammer

A Hammer has a small body at the top and a long bottom wick (at least 2× the body). It appears after a downtrend. It means: price fell sharply during the day, but buyers pushed it back up before the close. This is a bullish reversal signal.

  • Small body near the top of the candle
  • Long lower wick — the "handle" of the hammer
  • Little or no upper wick
  • Most reliable when it appears after a clear downtrend
🔨 Hammer example

TCS has fallen for 3 days. On day 4, it opens at ₹3,600, drops to ₹3,520 (long wick), then closes at ₹3,595. Small body, long bottom wick — a Hammer. Buyers stepped in at lower prices. A bounce upward often follows.

Pattern 3 — Engulfing

An Engulfing pattern is two candles. The second candle's body completely covers ("engulfs") the first candle's body. It shows a sudden shift in power.

  • Bullish Engulfing — a small red candle followed by a large green candle that covers it. Appears after a downtrend. Buyers took over.
  • Bearish Engulfing — a small green candle followed by a large red candle that covers it. Appears after an uptrend. Sellers took over.
📊 Engulfing example

HDFC Bank falls for 4 days. Day 5: small red candle, body from ₹1,640 to ₹1,630. Day 6: opens at ₹1,625, closes at ₹1,658 — a large green candle that fully covers day 5's body. Bullish engulfing. Strong signal that buyers are back in control.

💡 How to use patterns

Never act on a single pattern alone. Always check: (1) Is this pattern appearing at a key support or resistance level? (2) Is the overall trend in your favour? (3) Is volume higher than usual on the pattern candle? More confirmations = stronger signal.

09 Key Levels

Support & Resistance

Support and Resistance are price levels where the stock tends to stop and reverse. They are the most important concept in technical analysis — almost every trading decision involves them.

📈 NIFTY — support & resistance in action

What is Support?

Support is a price level where a falling stock tends to stop and bounce back up. Think of it as a floor. At this price, buyers are interested and ready to buy — so selling pressure slows down.

📌 Support example

NIFTY falls to 23,800 in January, bounces. Falls again in March to 23,800, bounces again. Falls in May to 23,800, bounces again. The level 23,800 is strong support — every time price reaches there, buyers come in. As a trader, you watch this level closely.

What is Resistance?

Resistance is a price level where a rising stock tends to stop and fall back down. Think of it as a ceiling. At this price, sellers are ready to sell — so buying pressure weakens.

📌 Resistance example

Infosys rises to ₹1,900, then falls. Rises again to ₹1,900, then falls. A third time — same story. The level ₹1,900 is strong resistance. Sellers keep showing up there. Until this level is broken, the stock struggles to go higher.

Support and Resistance flip roles

This is one of the most powerful ideas in technical analysis. When price breaks through a resistance level, that old resistance often becomes new support — and vice versa.

  • Resistance broken → becomes support. Price comes back to test it from above and bounces up
  • Support broken → becomes resistance. Price comes back to test it from below and falls again
🔄 Flip example

NIFTY was stuck below 25,000 for months (resistance). It finally breaks above 25,000 with strong volume. Two weeks later, NIFTY dips back to 25,000 and bounces up. The old resistance is now support. Many traders use this bounce as a buying opportunity.

How to identify key levels

  • Look for price levels where the stock has turned around multiple times in the past
  • Round numbers (₹1,000, ₹2,500, 24,000) often act as psychological support/resistance
  • Higher volume at a level makes it stronger — more people traded there
  • The more times a level has been tested, the more significant it is
💡 Practical tip

Open any stock on TradingView on the Daily chart. Draw a horizontal line at the price where you see multiple tops or bottoms. That's your support or resistance. Practice on NIFTY, Reliance, and TCS first — they have the clearest levels because so many traders watch them.

10 Direction

Trend

A trend is the general direction in which a stock or index is moving over time. There are three types: up, down, and sideways. The oldest rule in trading is: "The trend is your friend." Trade with it, not against it.

Uptrend

An uptrend is when price makes higher highs and higher lows over time. Each peak is higher than the last, and each dip is also higher than the last. Buyers are in control.

  • Each rally goes higher than the previous rally
  • Each pullback stays above the previous pullback low
  • Uptrends favour buying on dips (buying during small pullbacks)
📈 Uptrend example

NIFTY: peaks at 23,500 → dips to 23,000 → rises to 24,200 → dips to 23,700 → rises to 25,000. Each high is higher than the last. Each low is also higher. Classic uptrend. Traders who bought on each dip made money.

Downtrend

A downtrend is the opposite — lower highs and lower lows. Every rally fails to reach the previous high, and every dip goes lower than the last. Sellers are in control.

  • Each rally reaches a lower peak than the one before
  • Each dip falls below the previous dip
  • In a downtrend, avoid buying. Wait for a confirmed reversal before entering
📉 Downtrend example

A mid-cap stock: peaks at ₹500 → dips to ₹460 → rises to ₹485 → dips to ₹430 → rises to ₹455 → dips to ₹400. Every high is lower. Every low is lower. Buyers who "bought the dip" kept losing. Always check the trend before buying a falling stock.

Sideways (Range-bound)

A sideways trend (also called consolidation or a range) is when price moves back and forth between a clear top (resistance) and a clear bottom (support), going nowhere overall. Neither buyers nor sellers are winning.

  • Price bounces between a fixed ceiling and floor
  • Range traders buy near support and sell near resistance
  • Ranges often end in a breakout — a sharp move up or down out of the range
↔️ Sideways example

Wipro trades between ₹440 (support) and ₹465 (resistance) for 6 weeks. It bounces between these two levels repeatedly. Then one day it closes at ₹472 on high volume — a breakout. The sideways phase is over. Traders who spotted this entered early in the new uptrend.

📉📈 Three trend types — at a glance

How to identify a trend

Trend Type What you see on the chart What to do
Uptrend Higher highs + higher lows Look to buy on dips
Downtrend Lower highs + lower lows Stay out or wait for reversal
Sideways Price bouncing in a flat range Trade the range or wait for breakout
💡 The most common beginner mistake

Buying a stock just because it has fallen a lot — hoping it will "bounce back." If it's in a downtrend (lower highs, lower lows), there's no reason it must bounce. Always confirm the trend has actually reversed before buying. A downtrend ends when the stock finally makes a higher high for the first time.

11 Indicators

Indicators — RSI, MACD & Moving Averages

Indicators are maths-based tools that are drawn on top of (or below) a price chart. They help you answer three questions: Which direction is the trend? Is the stock overbought or oversold? Is momentum shifting? You don't need all of them — knowing three well is enough.

1 — Moving Average (MA)

A Moving Average smooths out price noise by showing the average closing price over a set number of days. Instead of seeing every zigzag, you see a clean line that shows the overall direction.

  • 20 MA — average of the last 20 days. Shows short-term direction
  • 50 MA — average of the last 50 days. Shows medium-term direction
  • 200 MA — average of the last 200 days. Shows long-term direction. The most watched level by big investors

How to read it: If price is above the MA, the trend is up. If price is below the MA, the trend is down. Simple as that.

Golden Cross & Death Cross: When the 50 MA crosses above the 200 MA, it's called a Golden Cross — a bullish signal. When it crosses below, it's a Death Cross — a bearish signal. These are watched globally.

📈 MA example

Say NIFTY is around 24,500 and its 200 MA is near 23,200 (example). Price is well above the 200 MA — the long-term trend is up. A trader uses the 50 MA as support: every time NIFTY dips to the 50 MA and bounces, they buy.

📈 Moving averages on a price chart — NIFTY example
🎯 One-line use case

Use the 200 MA to decide if a stock is in a long-term uptrend before buying — only buy stocks trading above their 200-day moving average.

2 — RSI (Relative Strength Index)

RSI measures how fast and how much a stock has moved recently. It gives you a number between 0 and 100. Think of it as a speedometer — it tells you if a stock has moved too fast in one direction and might be due for a pause or reversal.

  • RSI above 70 — stock is overbought. It has risen too fast. A pullback or pause is possible
  • RSI below 30 — stock is oversold. It has fallen too fast. A bounce is possible
  • RSI around 50 — neutral zone. No strong signal either way
🎯 RSI scale — what each zone means

Important: Overbought doesn't mean "sell immediately." A strong stock can stay overbought for a long time. RSI is most useful when combined with support/resistance levels.

📊 RSI example

Infosys has rallied 12% in 10 days. RSI hits 76 — overbought territory. The stock then flatlines for a week before dipping 4%. A swing trader who saw the RSI at 76 near a resistance level chose to take profits early rather than hold through the dip.

🎯 One-line use case

When a stock you want to buy has RSI below 35 and is sitting at a known support level — that combination is a strong signal to consider entering.

3 — MACD

MACD (say it: "Mac-Dee") stands for Moving Average Convergence Divergence. Don't worry about the name. Here's all you need to know: MACD tells you whether momentum is building up or fading.

The MACD panel has three parts:

  • MACD line — the faster line. Reacts quickly to price changes
  • Signal line — the slower line. Lags behind slightly
  • Histogram — bars that show the gap between the two lines. Bigger bars = stronger momentum

The main signal — the crossover:

  • MACD line crosses above Signal line → bullish signal. Momentum is turning up. Many traders use this as a buy trigger
  • MACD line crosses below Signal line → bearish signal. Momentum is turning down. Consider exiting or staying out
⚡ MACD example

HDFC Bank has been falling for 3 weeks. Then the MACD line (which was below the Signal line) crosses above the Signal line. The histogram bars start shrinking and flip green. A trader sees this as early evidence that sellers are losing control — and watches for a buy entry near support.

🎯 One-line use case

Use a MACD bullish crossover on the daily chart as a confirmation signal — enter a trade only after both the trend (price above MA) and momentum (MACD crossed up) agree.

Using all three together

Indicator What it answers Key signal
Moving Average What is the trend? Price above/below MA; Golden/Death Cross
RSI Is the stock stretched? Above 70 = overbought · Below 30 = oversold
MACD Is momentum shifting? MACD line crossing the Signal line
🔗 Combined signal example

Reliance is above its 200 MA (uptrend ✓). RSI is at 38 after a pullback (not overbought ✓). MACD just crossed bullish (momentum turning up ✓). All three agree — this is a high-quality setup. Three confirmations are far stronger than any single indicator alone.

Part 3 — Strategy & Mindset

12 Protection

Risk Management

Making money in the market is not just about picking the right stocks. It's about protecting yourself when you're wrong. Even the best traders lose on 4 out of 10 trades. Risk management is what keeps them alive for the other 6.

Rule 1 — Never risk too much on one trade

The 1–2% Rule — Never risk more than 1–2% of your total capital on a single trade. If you have ₹1,00,000 in your account, your maximum loss on any one trade should be ₹1,000–₹2,000. This way, even 10 losing trades in a row only costs you 10–20% — not your entire account.

📌 Position sizing example

Say you have ₹50,000. You buy Infosys at around ₹1,800 and decide your stop-loss is at approx. ₹1,755 (₹45 below). Risk per share = ₹45. If your max loss is ₹1,000 (2% of capital), you can buy ~22 shares (₹1,000 ÷ ₹45). That's position sizing — not buying "as many as you can afford."

Rule 2 — Stop Loss is not optional

A stop-loss is a pre-set price at which you exit a trade to limit your loss. You decide it before you enter — not after the stock is already falling. It removes emotion from the decision.

  • Place your stop-loss below a support level, not at a random number
  • Once set, do not move it lower just because you "hope" the stock will recover
  • Think of it as the cost of being wrong — a small, fixed fee
🛑 Stop-loss example

Say you buy TCS at around ₹3,900 with a stop-loss at approx. ₹3,820. TCS drops to ₹3,820 — you exit and lose roughly ₹80 per share. Painful, but controlled. Without a stop-loss, you might have held all the way down much further, hoping for a recovery that never came.

Rule 3 — Risk/Reward Ratio

Before entering any trade, ask: how much can I lose vs how much can I gain? A good trade has a minimum 1:2 risk/reward ratio — meaning if you risk ₹1, you should aim to make at least ₹2.

  • 1:1 — break even over time. Not worth it
  • 1:2 — you can be right only 40% of the time and still be profitable
  • 1:3 — excellent. Even winning 33% of trades keeps you in profit
⚖️ Risk/Reward example

Say you buy Reliance at around ₹2,900. Stop-loss at approx. ₹2,840 (risk ~₹60). Target around ₹3,020 (reward ~₹120). That's a 1:2 ratio. Even if you lose 5 trades and win 5, the 5 wins (~₹600) beat the 5 losses (~₹300). You profit ~₹300 overall with a 50% win rate.

⚖️ Risk/Reward — why 1:2 wins even at 50% win rate
💡 The golden rule

Cut your losses short. Let your winners run. Most beginners do the opposite — they sell winners quickly (afraid of losing gains) and hold losers for too long (hoping they'll recover). Flip this habit and your results will improve dramatically.

13 Approach

Investing vs Trading

Both involve buying stocks — but the mindset, time horizon, and skill set are completely different. Knowing which one suits you can save you years of frustration and money.

Long-Term Investing

  • You buy and hold stocks or index funds for years or decades
  • You rely on the company's business growth, not short-term price moves
  • Requires very little time — review your portfolio once a month is enough
  • Best tool: SIP into NIFTY 50 index fund. Low effort, historically strong returns
  • Tax rate: 12.5% LTCG — only applied after you hold for more than 1 year. Very tax-friendly for patient investors
📅 Investing example

In 2014, an investor put ₹10,000/month into a NIFTY 50 index fund via SIP. By 2024, the NIFTY had roughly tripled. Their ₹12 lakh invested grew to over ₹25 lakh — without picking a single stock or watching charts daily.

Trading

  • You aim to profit from short-term price movements — days, weeks, or even minutes
  • Requires active time — watching charts, managing trades, reviewing daily
  • Higher potential returns, but also higher risk and stress
  • Most beginners who try intraday trading lose money in the first year
  • Tax rate: 20% STCG — applied on profits from stocks held under 1 year. Significantly higher than long-term investing

Which one suits you?

Factor Investing Trading
Time needed 1–2 hrs/month 1–4 hrs/day
Skill needed Basic (fundamentals) High (TA + discipline)
Stress level Low High
Time horizon Years to decades Minutes to weeks
Best for Salaried professionals, beginners Full-time focused learners
💡 Honest advice for beginners

Start with long-term investing — a monthly SIP in a NIFTY 50 index fund. This builds wealth with near-zero effort. Only move to active trading after you've spent at least 6–12 months studying charts, practicing on paper trades, and understanding risk. Don't rush it.

14 Mindset

Psychology & Common Mistakes

Technical analysis can be learned in months. But controlling your emotions takes years. Most traders don't lose because of bad strategy — they lose because fear and greed cause them to abandon their strategy at exactly the wrong moment.

The two emotions that destroy traders

  • Fear — makes you exit winning trades too early, avoid good setups, and panic-sell during normal pullbacks
  • Greed — makes you hold losers hoping they'll recover, overtrade, and take oversized positions
🧠 The emotional spectrum — where are you?
😰 Fear in action

Say you buy Reliance at around ₹2,900 with a target of approx. ₹3,100. It reaches ₹2,980. You get nervous and sell early — locking in ~₹80 instead of waiting for ~₹200. Three days later it hits ₹3,110. Fear cost you roughly ₹120 per share.

🤑 Greed in action

You're down 8% on a stock. Instead of cutting the loss at your stop-loss level, you think "it'll come back." It falls another 15%. Now you're down 23% and paralysed. Greed (and hope) turned a small, planned loss into a big, unplanned one.

Common mistakes beginners make

  • No Plan — entering a trade without knowing your stop-loss or profit target in advance. This is how most beginner losses happen
  • Revenge Trading — placing a new impulsive trade right after a loss, hoping to "win it back" quickly. Almost always leads to a second, bigger loss
  • Overtrading — placing too many trades out of boredom or FOMO. Remember: not trading is sometimes the best trade
  • Moving Your Stop-Loss — shifting your stop-loss lower (or removing it) because you "feel" the stock will recover. It usually doesn't
  • Blind Tips — acting on WhatsApp or Telegram "hot tips" without understanding the reason. If you don't know why to buy, you won't know when to sell
  • No Diversification — putting all your money in one stock means one bad outcome can wipe out months of gains. Spread across at least 5–8 stocks
  • Obsessing Over P&L — checking your profit/loss every hour creates anxiety and leads to panic decisions. Trust your plan and check at end of day

Habits that build consistency

  • Write a trade journal — after every trade, note why you entered, what happened, and what you learned. The pattern of your mistakes will become obvious within weeks
  • Follow a checklist — before entering any trade, tick off: trend ✓, support/resistance ✓, stop-loss set ✓, risk/reward acceptable ✓
  • Accept that losses are part of the process — even a 60% win rate is excellent. Losses are not failures; unplanned losses are
  • Take breaks — after 2–3 losses in a row, step away from the screen for the day. Trading while frustrated leads to more losses
  • Review weekly, not daily — judge your performance over 20–30 trades, not 2–3
📓 Trade journal example

Date: 12 Mar  |  Stock: HDFC Bank  |  Entry: ~₹1,640  |  Stop: ~₹1,610  |  Target: ~₹1,700  |  Result: Stopped out at ~₹1,610, loss ~₹30/share  |  Lesson: Entered before RSI confirmed — was too early. Wait for the candle to close next time.

The right mindset in one sentence

Focus on following your process correctly, not on the outcome of any single trade. If you followed your rules and still lost — that's fine. If you broke your rules and won — that's dangerous. Consistency comes from process, not from luck.

💡 The best thing you can do right now

Before putting real money into any trade, spend 30 days paper trading — write down your entries, stops, and targets without actually placing orders. See how your strategy performs. Fix what's not working. Then trade small with real money. Patience at the start saves a lot of pain later.
